01

Overview

MOSU was a material-research driven design venture I built from concept to object to storefront, bringing together product design, creative storytelling, and digital commerce as a one-person lead system.

Scope
Product Design, Brand Direction, Ecommerce, Rendering
Role
Founder and Design Lead
02

Challenge

The challenge was to build a venture where making, environmental thinking, and public engagement reinforced one another rather than pulling in separate directions. That meant designing not just the objects, but also the logistical and social system around them, so the storefront, production process, and supporter-researcher community could operate as one connected structure.

03

Product system

The pieces were conceived as both design objects and artifacts of temporal process. Instead of treating products as fixed and untouched by time (allowing creators to ignore post-life responsibilities), the project considered how they might weather, erode, and transform. Texture and silhouette were shaped by material behavior, fabrication logic, and an intentional quietness, and some supporters even bought pieces specifically to let them age in soil or water into patina and surface change.

04

Brand and commerce

The storefront was designed as an extension of the project’s research rather than a neutral sales surface. Product framing, renders, and interface structure were used to make the process legible, giving equal weight to the objects, the material logic behind them, and the community forming around their lifecycle. The brand was also used as a platform for design competitions and external presentation, eventually being selected for sale by Buddy at the gift shop of the Chicago Cultural Center.

05

Storytelling assets

The visual direction needed to communicate not just what the pieces were, but the ethos behind them: temporality, openness, and material inquiry. Renders, studio process, and editorial framing were used to give the project the voice it needed to stay transparent and research-oriented. The brand was also kept alive by encouraging social media supporters to share direction suggestions and running a blog of interviews with artists and scientists exploring unique materials, and using those conversations to publish speculative pieces on the future of material design.

06

Outcome

MOSU became a way of testing how objects, research, and community could exist as one system rather than as separate layers of product, branding, and sales. The project treated material behavior, temporal change, and public participation as part of the design itself, resulting in a venture that made process visible, invited conversation, and framed commerce as one expression of a broader experimental practice.
